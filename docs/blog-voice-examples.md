## 1. The target blend, in one sentence

Earnestness of a working breeder, precision of a science writer, and
confidence of a columnist — with short sentences that earn the long
ones.

## 2. Reference writers (temporary picks — replace with Tennyson's)

### 2a. Ed Yong (science writing)

*Why:* Explains hard biology without dumbing down. Opens with a scene
or a question. Acknowledges complexity. Uses concrete numbers. Never
condescends.

**Excerpt (from *I Contain Multitudes*):**

> The world is teeming with organisms. There are millions of plants
> and animals. Somewhere between 10 and 100 million. But these are
> dwarfed, almost beyond comprehension, by microbes — the bacteria,
> viruses, archaea, and fungi that together make up the bulk of Earth's
> living tissue. Every animal you have ever seen, every plant, every
> fungus — each is a walking community. Your own body is home to
> trillions of bacteria. They are not hangers-on. You are their host.

What to copy: opening with scale and specificity, short sentences
building into longer ones, the payoff of "you are their host"
reframing the reader's sense of themselves.

### 2b. Patrick McKenzie / "patio11" (opinion with receipts)

*Why:* Writes with authority grounded in specific experience. Takes
strong positions and defends them with evidence, not vibes. Uses
first person without making every paragraph about himself. Good at
the "here's why the conventional wisdom is wrong" move.

**Excerpt (paraphrased in-style from *Bits About Money*):**

> Everyone in the industry knows credit cards exist because they make
> the customer feel good. Everyone is wrong. Credit cards exist because
> they solve an extraordinarily specific coordination problem between
> banks, merchants, and regulators — a problem that was not obvious
> until the 1950s, and the solution to which is the most elegant piece
> of financial plumbing in the modern economy. Customer psychology is
> at best the tenth most important thing. Let me show you the first
> nine.

What to copy: the flat contradiction of consensus ("Everyone is
wrong"), the specificity of the alternative ("the 1950s," "a
coordination problem"), the teacher's promise ("let me show you").

### 2c. Pangea Reptile blog (domain voice)

*Why:* Writes like an experienced breeder talking to other breeders.
Uses hobby jargon correctly (het, 66% possible het, line-bred vs.
proven). Admits uncertainty where the community is uncertain. Takes
photos of their own animals as evidence instead of using stock imagery.

**Excerpt (in-style, representative of the Pangea blog voice):**

> We've worked with the Cappuccino gene since 2021, and the one thing
> that still surprises us is how much the expression shifts between
> the first shed and the one-year mark. A hatchling that looks solid
> mocha can lighten into something that's barely distinguishable from
> a dark buckskin. If you're judging Cappuccino hets at eight weeks,
> stop. Wait until the animal has at least two full sheds under its
> belt. We have a box of photos proving it's worth the wait.

What to copy: "We've worked with," timeline-based authority ("since
2021," "at the one-year mark"), imperative advice grounded in
experience ("stop. Wait"), closing with social proof that isn't
boastful.

## 3. Non-examples (avoid this)

The following voices are **off-brand**. If a draft reads like any of
these, it gets rewritten.

- **Generic SEO listicle.** *"1. Choose the right morph. 2. Set up
  your enclosure. 3. Monitor humidity. These 10 steps will make you
  a successful breeder."* — No personality, no stakes, no
  specificity.
- **Medical-disclaimer voice.** *"It is important to consult a
  qualified veterinarian before making any changes to your
  animal's husbandry."* — Reads as liability-first, reader-second.
- **Influencer hype voice.** *"Crested geckos are ABSOLUTELY the
  most incredible pet you can own. You won't BELIEVE how easy
  they are."* — Emotion without content.
- **Textbook voice.** *"The Correlophus ciliatus species, commonly
  known as the crested gecko, is a member of the family
  Diplodactylidae…"* — Reads like Wikipedia. Save the taxonomy for
  the glossary.

## 4. Rhythm rule

Every post should pass this read-aloud test: if a human reads it out
loud, it should never sound like a machine wrote it. Short sentences
next to long ones. Occasional fragments. Occasional conjunctions to
open a sentence. A question the reader will answer in their head
before you answer it on the page.

If the draft reads smoothly at 1.5× speed but sounds lifeless at
1.0×, the rhythm is broken — rewrite.
