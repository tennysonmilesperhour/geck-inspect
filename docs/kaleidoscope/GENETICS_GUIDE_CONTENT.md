## Section 1: Introduction

**Title:** Understanding Crested Gecko Genetics

**Body:**

Crested gecko genetics has long been considered "random" or unpredictable compared to species like ball pythons or leopard geckos. This guide presents two modern frameworks that aim to change that: the Kaleidoscope Model of Inheritance (Foley & Donaldson, 2025) and the Foundation Genetics framework (LIL Monsters Reptiles).

Both frameworks reject the idea that crested gecko colors and patterns are random. Both ground their work in real chromatophore biology, the science of how pigment cells stack in reptile skin. They disagree on important details, especially around the Phantom phenotype, but they agree on the fundamental insight: crested gecko genetics is layered, and once you understand the layers, breeding outcomes become predictable in ranges.

This guide leans primarily on the Kaleidoscope Model because it provides a structured notation system that maps cleanly to a breeding prediction tool. We compare it to Foundation Genetics throughout for context.

Neither model has been confirmed by laboratory genetic research. Both are observational frameworks built on decades of breeding data. Both are open to revision as new evidence emerges.

**Source links:** Kaleidoscope PDF, Foundation Genetics PDF.

## Section 2: The four trait categories

**Title:** Pattern, Color, Coverage, Structure

**Body:**

The Kaleidoscope Model splits everything visible on a crested gecko into four categories.

**Color** is the pigment supply. Three independent base color genes (Yellow, Red, Black) determine what pigments are present and at what dosage.

**Pattern** is the layout. Two genes (Tiger and Pinstripe) push the colors around the body in opposite directions, vertically and horizontally.

**Coverage** is a special filter that takes one specific color (Yellow) and segregates it into the dorsal and lateral "Harlequin regions." This category is what creates the orange-and-white look most modern Harlequins display.

**Structure** is the underlying architecture: head shape, crest size, body proportions, scale arrangement. Structure is mostly polygenic and refined over generations of selective breeding.

Think of a crested gecko as a printed image. Color is the ink. Pattern is the stencil. Coverage is a filter that masks part of the print. Structure is the paper itself. Each category inherits independently and combines to produce the final phenotype.

## Section 3: Color — the Y/R/B base system

**Title:** Yellow, Red, and Black: The Three Base Colors

**Body:**

The Kaleidoscope Model proposes that crested geckos have three independent base color genes. Every gecko carries two alleles per base color, one inherited from each parent. We can write any gecko's base color as a triplet:

- The first number is Yellow alleles (0, 1, or 2)
- The second is Red alleles (0, 1, or 2)
- The third is Black alleles (0, 1, or 2)

A 2/2/2 gecko has all three base colors at full strength. A 0/2/0 is pure Red with no Yellow or Black contribution. A 0/0/0 has no pigment alleles at all and appears as a pale cream that does not "fire" the way a typical crested gecko does.

**Why the colors layer.** Pigment cells called chromatophores stack in the skin in a specific order. Yellow chromatophores sit on top, Red sits in the middle, and Black sits underneath. This is why pattern genes that disrupt the upper Yellow layer reveal Red or Black underneath, creating the depth and complexity seen in modern morphs.

**The 27 base color combinations.** With three independent genes each having three possible allele counts, the math gives 3^3 = 27 possible base color combinations. Coverage roughly doubles this number once it is applied. This is why nearly every crested gecko looks unique.

**A counterintuitive point.** More dominant alleles does not always mean darker. A pure Red gecko (0/2/0) is more vibrant than a Red gecko that also carries Yellow or Black, because the layering interferes. The most striking pure-color geckos are those where the other two base colors are absent.

**Confidence in this notation.** You almost never know a gecko's exact triplet from looking at it. You estimate from phenotype, then refine through breeding outcomes. Geck Inspect tracks confidence levels for each genotype assignment so users know when they are working from estimate vs. confirmed data.

## Section 4: Pattern — Tiger and Pinstripe forces

**Title:** The Antagonistic Pattern Forces

**Body:**

Tiger and Pinstripe are the two pattern genes in the Kaleidoscope Model. They behave with incomplete dominance, meaning a gecko with one copy looks different from a gecko with zero copies, and a gecko with two copies looks different again.

**Tiger is a vertical force.** Named for the vertical bands of contrasting color it creates between base colors, Tiger pushes pattern up and down across the body. The Yellow base color is most visibly affected, but Tiger's influence appears whenever there are contrasting colors to separate.

**Pinstripe is a horizontal force.** Pinstripe shows as parallel rows of enlarged scales running snout to tail along the dorsal region. Homozygous Pinstripes (two copies) often have an additional row along the mid-lateral area, called Quad Stripe. The pinstripe force runs horizontally, mostly confined to the dorsal area.

**They oppose each other.** This is the key insight. Tiger and Pinstripe interact antagonistically. The fullest expression of either gene appears when the other is absent or weak. When both are present, you get "breaks" in the pinstripe rows, which are not flaws but evidence of the Tiger gene pushing back against Pinstripe's horizontal flow.

**The two-gene Punnett produces nine unique combinations.** With two independent pattern genes each having three possible expressions (0, 1, or 2 copies), we get 9 visually distinct pattern outcomes ranging from "patternless" (0 copies of each) to maximum combined expression (2 copies of each).

**A testable prediction.** Pair an Extreme Harlequin (high Tiger, no Pinstripe) with a Tiger-less Full Pinstripe. The Kaleidoscope Model predicts all offspring will inherit one Tiger allele and one Pinstripe allele, producing intermediate expression of both forces. If breeders consistently observe this, the model holds. If they do not, the model needs revision.

**A note on Foundation Genetics' view.** The LIL Monsters framework treats Tiger as fixed in the population, meaning every crested gecko has at least some Tiger influence. Kaleidoscope disagrees, saying truly Tiger-less geckos exist. This is testable through breeding.

## Section 5: Coverage — the Harlequin/CvG hypothesis

**Title:** Why Phantom May Not Be Recessive

**Body:**

The most controversial part of the Kaleidoscope Model is its reframing of the Phantom phenotype. The traditional view says Phantom is a recessive gene that suppresses white and orange pattern. The Kaleidoscope Model says Phantom is not its own gene at all. It is the absence of a gene called Coverage Gene (CvG).

**The proposal in plain language.**

- Harlequin is one gene that segregates Yellow base color into specific dorsal and lateral regions, creating the "Harlequin pattern."
- CvG is a separate gene that travels with Harlequin (the two are completely linked on the same chromosome, meaning they almost never separate during reproduction).
- CvG is what controls the white pattern that accompanies Harlequin.
- A "non-phantom" gecko is one that has at least one Harlequin/CvG copy.
- A "phantom" gecko is one that lacks Coverage entirely.

**Phantom is the wild type.** In wild populations of crested geckos, over 90% of individuals would be classified as Phantom by today's hobby standards. Selective breeding has dramatically increased Coverage prevalence in the captive population.

**Why this matters for breeding.** Under the traditional "Phantom is recessive" model, phantom offspring should follow standard Mendelian recessive inheritance. Two visually identical "het phantoms" should produce 25% phantom offspring. But breeders have observed that some non-phantom pairings produce phantoms and others do not, even when the parents look similar. The Kaleidoscope Model explains this by tracking CvG copies separately from Harlequin copies.

**A specific prediction.** All non-phantom geckos that can directly produce phantoms when paired together will, when those phantoms appear, reveal an underlying Yellow base color that the Harlequin gene was previously masking. This holds even in geckos that look red-based or dark-based. The yellow was hidden, not absent.

**The (2/2/0) edge case.** A gecko homozygous for Yellow with no Red and homozygous for Black, paired with another (2/2/0), cannot directly produce phantom offspring under the Kaleidoscope Model. Both Yellow alleles must be impacted by Harlequin in such pairings. If a verified (2/2/0) × (2/2/0) pairing produces a phantom in a single generation, the model is falsified.

**Foundation Genetics disagrees.** The LIL Monsters framework holds that Phantom is a recessive trait that adds melanin and reduces white coloration. They acknowledge the gene "loses dominance" in certain areas (tail base, laterals), which the Kaleidoscope Model interprets as evidence the gene does not exist as described.

This debate is unresolved. Geck Inspect tracks both interpretations so users can record outcomes against both models.

## Section 6: The Perfect Recipe — 2/0/2

**Title:** Why Tri-Colors Come from a Specific Genotype

**Body:**

The 2/0/2 genotype produces the most striking Tri-Colors. Here is what is happening:

- Two copies of Yellow give the gecko a strong Yellow upper layer
- Zero copies of Red mean nothing competes between the Yellow and the Black
- Two copies of Black create a deep dark layer underneath
- Two copies of Harlequin redistribute the Yellow into the Harlequin regions, leaving the Black to show through where Harlequin "moves Yellow aside"
- When at least one Harlequin allele is paired with a strong CvG allele, you get the white pattern that completes the Tri-Color look

The "perfect" Tri-Color requires not just the right base color genotype but also high-quality CvG alleles. This is why two beautiful Tri-Colors paired together do not always produce equally beautiful Tri-Color offspring. The CvG allele quality varies, and lower-quality CvG produces less white pattern.

This explains a frustration many breeders have experienced: a clutch from two visually stunning parents producing offspring that range widely in Tri-Color quality. The base color genotype is reliable. The pattern is reliable. But the white pattern strength depends on CvG allele quality, which is harder to track and improve in a single generation.

## Section 7: Lavender reframed

**Title:** Lavender Is Not "Hypo Black"

**Body:**

The hobby has long described Lavender as a "Hypo Black Base" expression, suggesting a gene that lightens the Black base color. The Kaleidoscope Model proposes a different explanation.

**The Kaleidoscope view.** Lavender is a phenotype that arises from specific base color combinations, most notably (2/0/2) and (2/1/1), combined with two Harlequin genes (each impacting a separate Yellow allele), low Tiger expression, and strong Pinstripe presence.

The combination produces the soft, slightly purple-tinted appearance Lavenders are known for. The lightness comes not from a gene that lightens Black, but from the specific way Yellow gets segregated by Harlequin while Pinstripe organizes the remaining pattern.

**A useful prediction.** Lavenders are known for producing exceptional Yellow and Red offspring from seemingly unexpected pairings. The Kaleidoscope Model explains why: Lavenders typically carry Yellow and Red alleles, and the absent or reduced Tiger creates a "cleaner" expression of those base colors. When Harlequin is not inherited by an offspring, the Yellow suppression lifts, and the offspring can express the homozygous Yellow vibrantly.

**A note on Tangerine.** The model authors mention a separate Tangerine pigment that appears to act on the Yellow base color, contributing to Lavender variations. They note this will be discussed in future texts. We will update this section as their work progresses.

## Section 8: True Hypo base colors

**Title:** Pale Yellow, Pink, Steel Grey, and Pure Cream

**Body:**

The Kaleidoscope Model proposes that "True Hypo" base colors are not the result of a single hypomelanism gene. They are the result of careful selective breeding to isolate base color alleles in heterozygous form, free from other base color influence.

**The four True Hypo phenotypes:**

- **Pale Yellow** (1/0/0): one copy of Yellow, no Red, no Black. A delicate, soft yellow.
- **Pink** (0/1/0): one copy of Red, no Yellow, no Black. A genuine pink rather than a faded red.
- **Steel Grey** (0/0/1): one copy of Black, no Yellow, no Red. A clean grey rather than a muddy brown.
- **Pure Cream** (0/0/0): no pigment alleles at all. A near-white phenotype that does not "fire" the way typical crested geckos do.

**Why selective breeding produced these.** Wild crested geckos benefit from camouflage, so natural selection favors muddy combinations of multiple base colors. The pure forms required generations of breeders identifying and pairing individuals with isolated base colors.

**Why this matters.** Under the Kaleidoscope Model, "Hypo" is not a gene that can be added to a gecko. It is a state of base color isolation that must be bred for. Breeders cannot acquire a "Hypo" trait the way they might acquire an axanthic carrier. They must build it through line breeding, generation by generation.

**A test of the model.** Pairing two True Hypo individuals of different base colors should produce offspring resembling either parent or showing combinations of their base colors. It should not produce a single uniform "Hypo" intermediate. This has been observed across hobby lines and supports the Kaleidoscope explanation.

## Section 9: Comparison with Foundation Genetics

**Title:** Two Frameworks, One Species

**Body:**

The Kaleidoscope Model and the Foundation Genetics framework (LIL Monsters Reptiles) are the two most rigorous attempts to clean up crested gecko genetics. They agree on a great deal and disagree on specific points.

**What they share:**

- Both reject "morphs are random" framing.
- Both insist on standardized vocabulary using real genetic terms (alleles, homozygous, heterozygous, polygenic, incomplete dominance).
- Both ground their claims in chromatophore biology and the layered pigment system.
- Both treat Tiger and Pinstripe as real genetic forces.

**Key disagreements:**

| Topic | Foundation Genetics | Kaleidoscope |
|---|---|---|
| Phantom | Recessive gene, suppresses white pattern | Absence of CvG gene, not a gene itself |
| Tiger | Fixed in the population, every gecko has some | Discrete Mendelian gene, can be fully absent |
| Harlequin | A morph with allelic incomplete-dominant components | One gene linked to a separate Coverage Gene (CvG) |
| Base color | Mentioned but not developed into a full notation system | Y/R/B triplet system from 0/0/0 to 2/2/2 |

**How to use both.** These frameworks are not actually mutually exclusive in practice. Foundation Genetics provides the better educational vocabulary and definitions. Kaleidoscope provides the better predictive notation system for actual breeding work.

In Geck Inspect, the calculator can run predictions in both modes side by side. Users record their actual breeding outcomes, and the app compares each model's predictions against reality over time. The result is a community-built dataset that can help resolve the open questions.

## Section 10: Using this in your breeding program

**Title:** From Theory to Practice

**Body:**

The genetics guide is an educational reference. The actual tools live elsewhere in Geck Inspect:

**Genotype editor (gecko detail page).** For each gecko in your collection, you can record the Kaleidoscope genotype: Y/R/B base color triplet, Tiger and Pinstripe allele counts, Harlequin and CvG status. Each section has a confidence level so you can mark whether the genotype is confirmed by breeding outcomes, estimated from phenotype, or estimated from lineage.

**Breeding calculator.** For any two geckos in your collection (or a hypothetical pair), the calculator predicts offspring phenotype distributions. Switch between Classic mode (using established morph tags) and Kaleidoscope mode (using the Y/R/B notation) to see how predictions differ.

**Prediction tracking.** When you record a clutch and its offspring, the app scores how accurate each model's predictions were for that specific pairing. Over many clutches, this builds a personal accuracy log. Aggregate (anonymized) data across all Geck Inspect users contributes to the broader community understanding of which model's predictions hold up.

**A practical workflow:**

1. For each breeder in your collection, fill in the Kaleidoscope genotype to the best of your ability. Mark confidence honestly.
2. Before a planned pairing, run the calculator in both modes and review the predicted phenotype distribution.
3. After hatching, record the actual offspring phenotypes.
4. Review the prediction accuracy report after the season. Note which pairings were predicted accurately and which were not.
5. Use the accuracy patterns to refine your genotype confidence assignments over time.

**A cautionary note.** Both inheritance models are simplifications. Real breeding outcomes are influenced by allele expression quality (the "gene stacking" history of the parent's lineage), polygenic factors in structure, and gene interactions not yet captured by either model. Use the predictions as informed estimates, not certainties.
